\begin{abstract}
Verifiable reward post-training improves tokenized video world models by scoring groups of decoded candidate futures against ground-truth frames and reinforcing higher-reward candidates with GRPO. We identify two structural mismatches in this loop. First, the raw target is not exactly reachable through a lossy tokenizer--decoder pair, and its reconstruction residual can change the within-group ordering consumed by GRPO. Second, a sequence-level advantage assigns identical credit to all autoregressive future-frame token blocks. We propose RC-GRPO, which compares decoded candidates with the tokenizer-reachable target $D(E(s))$ and assigns group-normalized temporal-return advantages to individual future-frame blocks, while leaving the tokenizer, decoder, and group-relative update unchanged. Pairwise reward disagreements follow the closed-form prediction $\arccos(\rho)/\pi$, and rescoring identical candidate groups with the reachable target reduces the measured disagreement. Under a fixed held-out multi-step RT-1 protocol, reconstruction calibration lowers final LPIPS and MSE relative to the raw verifier in five of five paired seeds. Temporal-return credit obtains lower mean full-rollout and last-frame LPIPS than sequence-level calibrated GRPO in four of five seeds, with increasingly favorable deltas at later horizons. The full method gives the best mean LPIPS, last-frame LPIPS, and MSE among our trained variants and evaluates below the public RLVR-World checkpoint on all five seeds under the same held-out evaluation protocol.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Video world models predict future visual observations conditioned on past frames and actions, providing sampleable environment models for robotics, interactive simulation, and model-based reinforcement learning \citep{worldmodels,rssm,dreamerv3,dws}. A common implementation tokenizes visual observations and casts action-conditioned video prediction as sequence modeling: frames are encoded into discrete visual tokens, an autoregressive model predicts future tokens, and a decoder maps generated tokens back to images \citep{videogpt,magvit,ivideogpt,fsq}. Recent verifiable reward frameworks further suggest that these models can be improved after pre-training by sampling a group of candidate futures, scoring decoded predictions against ground-truth frames, and applying GRPO to reinforce candidates with higher within-group rewards \citep{rlvrworld,grpo}. This pipeline is attractive because it optimizes against measurable prediction errors rather than a learned critic.

This paper studies a narrower but important question: when the world model is tokenized, is the decoded full-reference reward the right signal for GRPO, especially in multi-step rollouts? We find two structural mismatches. First, post-decode verifiers compare candidates with raw ground truth even though the ground truth is not exactly reachable through the tokenizer--decoder pair. Second, multi-step GRPO often collapses a rollout into one sequence-level advantage, even though video prediction is autoregressive and frame errors propagate across horizons. These mismatches are easy to miss because the training loop remains verifiable: every candidate is still compared with a known target.

The first failure mode is verifier-side. A lossy visual tokenizer imposes an irreducible encode-decode residual on the ground-truth frame. We call this residual the reconstruction floor. It is not caused by any sampled candidate, but post-decode metrics such as MSE, SSIM, and LPIPS still include it when scoring decoded predictions \citep{ssim,lpips}. For ordinary evaluation, this is a reconstruction-quality limit of the tokenizer \citep{vqvae,vqgan,perceptiondistortion}. For GRPO, it is a ranking problem: group normalization removes any candidate-independent offset, so the floor's mean alone cannot bias the update; what corrupts training is the candidate-dependent component of the reconstruction residual, which reorders candidates within the sample group and changes which of them receives positive advantage.

The second failure mode is temporal. In a multi-step video rollout, early generated frames become the context for later predictions. A late error can therefore reflect earlier choices, later choices, or their interaction. Sequence-level GRPO ignores this structure by assigning the same normalized advantage to all generated tokens in the rollout. Fine-grained credit assignment has been explored for GRPO-style training in language reasoning and generative trajectories \citep{grpolambda,spo,sdgrpo,stepwiseflowgrpo,dapo}, but tokenized video has a different natural unit of credit: a future frame is represented by a block of visual tokens and evaluated by a frame-level verifier.

We propose \emph{RC-GRPO}, a reconstruction-calibrated temporal credit assignment method for tokenized video world models. RC-GRPO keeps the sample-group, verifiable-reward, and group-relative update structure intact, but changes the target and the credit unit. For verifier calibration, it replaces the raw ground-truth frame with the tokenizer-reachable target $\tilde{s}_t = D(E(s_t))$, so decoded predictions are compared in the same reachable image space. For temporal credit assignment, it computes frame-level rewards in multi-step rollouts and forms temporal-return advantages for each future-frame token block. Early tokens can then receive credit through later frame quality, while horizon-specific prediction errors are no longer mixed into a single scalar advantage.

Our contributions are twofold. First, we identify reconstruction-induced rank corruption in tokenized video GRPO and introduce a reconstruction-calibrated (RC) verifier that replaces the raw target with its tokenizer-reachable reconstruction. Pairwise reward disagreements follow the predicted $\arccos(\rho)/\pi$ relation across three reward spaces; on identical candidate groups, RC reduces disagreement with a pre-decode reference, and it lowers final LPIPS and MSE relative to the raw verifier in all five paired training seeds. Second, we introduce temporal-return GRPO for multi-step video rollouts. It assigns future-frame quality back to the token blocks that can affect it, obtains lower mean full-rollout and last-frame LPIPS than sequence-level RC in four of five seeds, and exhibits increasingly favorable deltas at later horizons. Controlled negative results bound these claims: adding further pre- or post-decode reward components does not pass our offline admission tests, and the evidence supports full-reference fidelity rather than a general improvement in motion or distributional realism.

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Tokenized video world models.}
World models learn predictive environment dynamics that can support planning, control, and simulation from visual observations \citep{worldmodels,rssm,dreamerv2,dreamerv3}. Recent video generative models extend this idea by modeling future visual observations directly, often through tokenized sequence modeling. VideoGPT, MAGVIT, iVideoGPT, Genie, and pre-trained video simulators show that discrete visual tokens and autoregressive or masked sequence models can support scalable video prediction and interactive generation \citep{videogpt,magvit,ivideogpt,genie,dws}. These works mainly study how to build or pre-train a sampleable video world model. Our focus is different: given a tokenized video world model and a verifiable post-training loop, we study whether the decoded verifier target and the sequence-level GRPO update match the structure of tokenized multi-step video prediction.

\paragraph{Verifiable reward post-training.}
Verifiable reward training samples multiple candidate outputs for the same context, scores them with an externally computable reward, and applies group-relative policy updates without a learned value function. RLVR-World instantiates this idea for world models, while GRPO and related RLVR-style methods have been widely explored in language reasoning and post-training \citep{rlvrworld,grpo,deepseekr1}. Recent variants analyze length bias, clipping behavior, large-scale optimization, classification-style rank labels, and adaptive policy objectives \citep{drgrpo,dapo,gspo,realrlvr,sapo}. We do not treat these optimizer variants as direct solutions to tokenized video post-training. In our setting, the verifier itself can corrupt within-group rankings because decoded candidates are compared with targets that are not exactly reachable through the tokenizer-decoder pair. This shifts the relevant intervention from a generic optimizer swap to video-structured verifier calibration.

\paragraph{Visual tokenizers and evaluation metrics.}
Discrete visual representation learning compresses images or videos into token sequences that can be modeled by transformers, but lossy tokenization introduces an encode-decode reconstruction limit \citep{vqvae,vqgan,fsq}. This limit is closely related to the broader perception-distortion tradeoff in image reconstruction \citep{perceptiondistortion}. Full-reference metrics such as SSIM and LPIPS are commonly used to evaluate per-sample fidelity, whereas FID, KID, FVD, DINO-based distances, precision/recall, and density/coverage evaluate distribution-level fidelity and diversity \citep{ssim,lpips,fid,kid,fvd,dinov2,precisionrecall,densitycoverage}. These metrics are usually used for reporting generation quality. We instead ask what happens when a post-decode full-reference metric becomes the reward consumed by GRPO: distribution-level metrics do not directly provide a per-candidate verifier, while post-decode per-sample metrics inherit the tokenizer reconstruction floor and can alter candidate rankings.

\paragraph{Fine-grained credit assignment.}
Sequence-level advantages can be too coarse when a generation trajectory contains multiple reasoning steps, semantic segments, or generation stages. Recent GRPO-style work addresses this issue through eligibility-trace credit assignment, segment-level policy optimization, verifiable segment decomposition, stepwise credit for flow-matching generation, and token-level loss shaping in large-scale RL systems \citep{grpolambda,spo,sdgrpo,stepwiseflowgrpo,dapo}. RC-GRPO adapts this principle to tokenized video rather than reusing token-level or denoising-step credit directly. In a video rollout, the natural credit unit is a future-frame block of visual tokens, and each block is scored by a frame-level full-reference verifier. This motivates temporal-return advantages over frame blocks after reconstruction-calibrated verifier targets, rather than a task-agnostic GRPO objective.

\section{Preliminaries}

\paragraph{Problem setup.}
We study verifiable post-training for action-conditioned tokenized video world models. Given a context $q_t$ consisting of past observations and actions, the model predicts future visual states $s_{t+1:t+H}$. A visual tokenizer $E$ maps each frame to discrete visual tokens, and a decoder $D$ maps generated tokens back to image space. We write the world model as an autoregressive policy $\pi_\theta$ over future visual tokens conditioned on $q_t$ and previously generated tokens. This paper does not modify the tokenizer, decoder, or backbone world model; it studies whether the verifier target and the GRPO update are aligned with this tokenized generation interface.

\paragraph{Verifiable reward training.}
In an RLVR-style update, the policy samples a group of $G$ candidate futures for the same context. Each candidate token sequence is decoded into a visual prediction $\hat{s}_i$, and a verifier assigns a reward $R_i$ by comparing the prediction with a target frame. GRPO normalizes rewards within the sample group,
\begin{equation}
A_i=\frac{R_i-\mu_R}{\sigma_R+\epsilon},
\label{eq:groupnorm}
\end{equation}
and uses $A_i$ to weight the log-probability of the sampled tokens. Because the normalization is group-relative, the effective training signal is not the absolute reward value alone, but the relative ordering and spacing of candidates within the group.

\paragraph{Multi-step rollouts.}
Single-step prediction evaluates one future frame. In a multi-step rollout, the model generates a sequence of future-frame token blocks, and decoded early frames become part of the context for later predictions. Let $r_{i,u}$ denote the frame-level reward of candidate $i$ at future horizon $u$; following the multi-step reward convention of RLVR-World \citep{rlvrworld}, the first future frame carries no direct action-conditioned reward, so $r_{i,1}=0$. A standard sequence-level GRPO baseline aggregates frame rewards into one rollout-level scalar,
\begin{equation}
R_i^{\mathrm{seq}}=\frac{1}{H-1}\sum_{u=2}^{H}r_{i,u},
\label{eq:seqreward}
\end{equation}
and broadcasts the resulting advantage to all generated tokens in the rollout. This defines the credit-assignment problem studied in this paper: tokenized video prediction is autoregressive across future frames, but a single sequence-level advantage cannot distinguish the responsibility of different future-frame token blocks.

\paragraph{Overview.}
RC-GRPO keeps the RLVR-style sample group, verifiable reward, and group-relative update intact, but changes two parts of the training signal that are specific to tokenized video. First, decoded predictions are compared with a tokenizer-reachable target rather than with the raw ground-truth image. Second, in multi-step rollouts, the advantage is assigned to future-frame token blocks through temporal returns rather than broadcast as one rollout-level scalar. The method therefore addresses verifier-side target mismatch and rollout-side credit mismatch without changing the tokenizer, decoder, or autoregressive world-model architecture.

\paragraph{Reconstruction-calibrated verifier.}
For a full-reference distance $d$, a lossy tokenizer induces a reconstruction floor
\begin{equation}
\phi_{\mathrm{tok}}^{(d)}
=
\mathrm{E}_{s_t}
\left[d(D(E(s_t)),s_t)\right].
\end{equation}
This term is not caused by a sampled candidate, but it is included when a post-decode verifier compares a decoded prediction with the raw frame. For a candidate group, let $\delta_i=r_i^{\mathrm{raw}}-r_i^{\mathrm{reach}}$, $c=G^{-1}\sum_i\delta_i$, and $\eta_i=\delta_i-c$. Then $r_i^{\mathrm{raw}}=r_i^{\mathrm{reach}}+c+\eta_i$. Group centering removes $c$, but not the candidate-dependent $\eta_i$; the latter can therefore change within-group ranks and spacings. RC-GRPO removes this target mismatch by replacing the raw target $s_t$ with the tokenizer-reachable target
\begin{equation}
\tilde{s}_t = D(E(s_t)).
\end{equation}
Given a decoded prediction $\hat{s}_{i,t}$, the reconstruction-calibrated reward is
\begin{equation}
r_{i,t}^{\mathrm{RC}}
=
-d(\hat{s}_{i,t},\tilde{s}_t).
\end{equation}
In our multi-step experiments we instantiate $d$ with the RLVR-World full-reference evaluator,
\begin{equation}
r_{i,t}^{\mathrm{RC}}
=
-\left[
\mathrm{MSE}(\hat{s}_{i,t},\tilde{s}_t)
+
\mathrm{LPIPS}(\hat{s}_{i,t},\tilde{s}_t)
\right].
\end{equation}
The raw verifier used for comparison replaces $\tilde{s}_t$ with $s_t$. The target remains verifiable because $\tilde{s}_t$ is deterministically obtained from the ground-truth frame and the frozen tokenizer; the change is that reward construction compares candidates in the image space reachable by the tokenizer--decoder pair. Final evaluation remains against the raw frame $s_t$, so the calibrated target cannot improve a reported metric by definition alone.

\paragraph{Temporal-return GRPO.}
Let a candidate rollout contain $H$ future-frame token blocks $o_{i,t,1:N}$, where $N$ is the number of visual tokens per frame block. Standard sequence-level GRPO assigns the group-normalized advantage of the rollout-level scalar $R_i^{\mathrm{seq}}$ in Eq.~\ref{eq:seqreward} to every generated token. RC-GRPO instead computes a temporal return for each future-frame block,
\begin{equation}
G_{i,t}
=
\sum_{u=t}^{H}
\beta^{u-t} r_{i,u}^{\mathrm{RC}},
\end{equation}
where $\beta$ is a temporal discount. The first future frame has no direct reward ($r_{i,1}=0$, as in the preliminaries), but it can still receive credit through later rewards in $G_{i,1}$. For each horizon $t$, we normalize returns across the candidates in the same sample group:
\begin{equation}
A_{i,t}^{\mathrm{return}}
=
\frac{G_{i,t}-\mu_t}{\sigma_t+\epsilon},
\end{equation}
where $\mu_t$ and $\sigma_t$ are computed over $\{G_{i,t}\}_{i=1}^{G}$.

\paragraph{Optimization.}
The temporal-return advantage weights the log-probability of the corresponding future-frame token block. Let
\begin{equation}
\ell_{i,t}
=
\sum_{\tau=1}^{N}
\log
\pi_{\theta}
\left(o_{i,t,\tau}\mid h_{i,t,\tau}\right),
\end{equation}
where $h_{i,t,\tau}$ denotes the autoregressive context of token $\tau$ in block $t$: the window context, the injected actions, and all previously generated tokens. The policy-gradient loss is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{RCG}}
=
-\frac{1}{G}
\sum_{i=1}^{G}
\sum_{t=1}^{H}
A_{i,t}^{\mathrm{return}}\ell_{i,t}.
\end{equation}
If $A_{i,t}^{\mathrm{return}}$ is replaced by the same sequence-level advantage for all $t$, the objective reduces to the sequence-level GRPO baseline. In implementation, multi-step training also uses a small reference-policy anchor,
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}
=
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{RCG}}
+
\lambda_{\mathrm{KL}}
\widehat{D}_{\mathrm{KL}}
\left(\pi_\theta\|\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}\right),
\end{equation}
where $\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}$ is the frozen base world model and $\widehat{D}_{\mathrm{KL}}$ is a per-token log-probability-difference estimate on the sampled rollout tokens. Each sampled group is consumed by exactly one gradient step, so the update remains on-policy and requires no importance ratio or clipping. The anchor is applied consistently across multi-step arms to reduce policy drift; horizon-aware variants and gain-shaped rewards are treated as ablations rather than as part of the main method.

\section{Experiments}

\paragraph{Experimental setup.}
We evaluated RC-GRPO on the RT-1 	exttt{fractal20220817} action-conditioned video data \citep{rt1} using the public RLVR-World multi-step base checkpoint and its fixed visual tokenizer \citep{rlvrworld}. The fixed split contains 24 training windows and 8 held-out evaluation windows; each window has one context frame and $H=7$ future frames. At every update, the policy samples $G=16$ candidate rollouts with temperature $1.0$ and top-$k=100$. Unless otherwise stated, multi-step runs use 30 optimization steps, learning rate $10^{-5}$, two sampled windows per step, evaluation every 10 steps, temporal discount $\beta=0.95$, advantage normalization $\epsilon=10^{-6}$, and a reference-policy anchor with $\lambda_{\mathrm{KL}}=0.001$. Because the first future frame carries no direct reward, per-horizon evaluation covers $t=2,\dots,7$, and LPIPS-last reports horizon 7. We compare the base checkpoint, the public multi-step RLVR-World checkpoint, raw and RC sequence-level GRPO, and temporal-credit variants; every trained arm uses paired seeds 0--4. We fixed the 30-step budget for all main comparisons after a preliminary stability study and report the final checkpoint, without selecting the best step. All held-out metrics compare predictions with raw future frames, never with reconstructed targets; lower LPIPS, LPIPS-last, and MSE indicate better full-reference fidelity.

\paragraph{Rank disagreements match the verifier-noise prediction.}
Before evaluating training outcomes, we tested whether reconstruction-induced reward variation changes the within-group ordering consumed by GRPO. For each candidate group, we correlate a decoded reward with a pre-decode code-space score of the same candidates and denote the correlation by $\rho$. The pre-decode score is not treated as ground-truth quality; it is a diagnostic reference that does not pass through the decoder. Under a jointly Gaussian score model, the probability that a candidate pair is ordered differently is the bivariate-Gaussian orthant probability $\arccos(\rho)/\pi$. Figure~\ref{fig:rank_flip} shows that this prediction tracks observed pairwise disagreement for LPIPS, MSE, and SSIM. We then rescore identical groups with RC. Across 184 held-out contexts and five independently sampled candidate groups per context (920 groups in total), aggregate disagreement decreases for LPIPS ($0.162\rightarrow0.151$), MSE ($0.181\rightarrow0.168$), and MSE+LPIPS ($0.160\rightarrow0.149$); all five generation repetitions show the same decrease, and the prediction remains within $0.005$ of the observed rate before and after calibration. Because contexts repeat across generation runs, we treat these values as a five-repetition diagnostic rather than 920 independent trials. Together, the results show that reachable-target calibration reduces measured rank disagreement; they do not claim that the code-space ordering is uniquely correct.

\paragraph{Main results.}
Table~\ref{tab:main_multistep} and Figure~\ref{fig:msp_learning} show that reconstruction calibration transfers to multi-step rollouts. On paired seeds 0--4, replacing the raw target with its tokenizer-reachable reconstruction lowers LPIPS by $0.0073 \pm 0.0099$ (5/5 seeds), LPIPS-last by $0.0130 \pm 0.0212$ (4/5), and MSE by $0.0010 \pm 0.0012$ (5/5). The paired $t$ statistics ($-1.7$, $-1.4$, and $-1.8$) are not significant at $n=5$, so these are directional multi-seed results rather than significance claims. Full RC-GRPO gives the lowest mean final values on all three metrics. Under our held-out evaluation protocol, every full-method seed also scores below the single public RLVR-World checkpoint on LPIPS, LPIPS-last, and MSE; this comparison does not imply matched training recipes.

\paragraph{Per-horizon analysis.}
Figure~\ref{fig:horizon_delta} isolates the GRPO-side contribution by comparing temporal-return RC-GRPO with sequence-level RC-GRPO. Over 30 seed--horizon cells (five seeds, horizons 2--7), temporal-return credit lowers LPIPS on 22/30 cells, MSE on 20/30, and MAE on 20/30, while increasing PSNR and SSIM on 21/30 each. Because horizons within a seed are correlated, these tallies show directional consistency rather than 30 independent trials. The mean LPIPS delta grows from $-0.0016$ at horizon 2 to $-0.0041$ at horizon 7; the latter comparison has paired $t=-2.0$ over five seeds and remains short of conventional significance. This horizon-dependent signature is consistent with assigning later-frame quality back to earlier token blocks, but does not by itself identify a causal interaction between the two method components.

\paragraph{Ablations and negative results.}
Table~\ref{tab:ablation_multistep} summarizes variants around the main method. Replacing sequence-level RC with temporal returns lowers mean LPIPS by $0.0029$ and LPIPS-last by $0.0041$ in 4/5 paired seeds, although neither comparison is significant at $n=5$. The frame-advantage control isolates the return structure: normalizing each horizon independently without accumulating future rewards is substantially worse than temporal-return RC (LPIPS $+0.0105$, paired $t=+6.2$; LPIPS-last $+0.0109$, $t=+5.1$; 0/5 wins) and is also worse than sequence-level RC. Thus, finer normalization alone is insufficient; retaining future rewards in each block's return is structurally important in this protocol. Gain shaping and horizon-aware KL do not improve temporal-return RC. Offline admission tests likewise assign zero marginal weight to two pre-decode signals and find no margin-stable gain from four additional post-decode metrics, so neither metric proliferation nor those generic variants enter the final method. We also do not claim broad dynamic-quality improvement: the dynamic-direction proxy dmotion improves on only 14/30 seed--horizon cells. The supported claim is full-reference multi-step fidelity.

\paragraph{Reward-independent readout.}
As a preliminary distribution-level check (seed 0, evaluation only), we embed predicted frames with DINOv2 and compute Fr\'echet and kernel distances against real held-out frames. Every post-trained checkpoint, including the public RLVR-World checkpoint, has worse KID than the base model (base $5.1$, RLVR-World $6.6$, raw sequence GRPO $13.2$, sequence RC $12.4$, temporal-return RC $9.8$). This single-seed association suggests that improved per-sample fidelity need not preserve distributional realism, but it is insufficient to establish a general trade-off. We therefore report it only as a preliminary boundary result.

\section{Conclusion}

Post-decode verifiers and sequence-level advantages each overlook structure in tokenized video: the raw target lies outside the tokenizer-reachable image space, and one scalar advantage cannot distinguish the responsibility of autoregressive future-frame blocks. RC-GRPO modifies these two interfaces. Reachable-target calibration reduces measured disagreement with a pre-decode diagnostic reference and lowers final fidelity errors relative to the raw verifier across paired seeds. Temporal-return credit gives the best mean final fidelity in our comparison and shows increasingly favorable later-horizon deltas, while the direct return-versus-sequence comparison remains statistically underpowered at five seeds. The evidence is bounded to one action-conditioned prediction interface: longer training can drift, dmotion does not improve, and a single-seed distributional check worsens for every post-trained arm. Within this scope, the results support aligning verifiers and credit units with the tokenized video generation process.
